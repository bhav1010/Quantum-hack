Section 01

WHAT IS
POLARIZATION?

Light is an electromagnetic wave. Polarization describes the direction that the electric field oscillates as the wave travels through space.

LIVE — Transverse oscillation of the electric field vector. Ex (blue) and Ey (pink) components combine into the total polarization state.
🌊
Unpolarized Light
In natural light (like the sun), photons vibrate in all possible directions perpendicular to travel. The electric field oscillates randomly — we say the light is unpolarized. When averaged over time, there's no preferred direction.
↕
Linear Polarization
After passing through a linear polarizer, the electric field is confined to oscillate along a single axis. We describe this by a polarization angle θ: 0° = horizontal, 90° = vertical, 45° = diagonal.
🌀
Circular Polarization
When two equal-amplitude, orthogonal components have a 90° phase difference (δ = π/2), the E-field tip traces a circle over time. It can be Left Circular (LCP) or Right Circular (RCP) depending on the sign of δ.
⬡
Elliptical Polarization
The most general case — the E-field tip traces an ellipse. Linear and circular polarization are just special cases. Described by two amplitudes (Ax, Ay) and a phase difference δ between Ex and Ey components.

01
💡 Laser Source
A coherent, monochromatic light source. Produces a beam with a well-defined wavelength and phase. In our lab we used a red diode laser (λ ≈ 650 nm). The output is often already partially polarized — we treat it as the initial Jones vector E_in.
E_in = [Ex, Ey]ᵀ
e.g. horizontal:
E = [1, 0]ᵀ
02
🔆 Linear Polarizer
A polarizing filter that only transmits the component of E-field along its transmission axis. Unpolarized light becomes linearly polarized. When stacked at angle θ to a previous polarizer, intensity follows Malus' Law. The polarizer axis is the key control in our experiment.
M_pol(θ) =
[[cos²θ, cosθ sinθ],
 [cosθ sinθ, sin²θ]]
03
⬡ Quarter-Wave Plate (QWP)
Introduces a 90° (λ/4) phase retardation between the fast and slow axes. Its key effect: linearly polarized light at 45° to the fast axis becomes circularly polarized. Used to convert between linear and circular polarization states. Fast axis orientation α controls the transformation.
Γ = 90° (λ/4)
Linear (45°) → Circular
M = R(-α)·diag(e^{-iπ/4},
      e^{+iπ/4})·R(α)
04
◈ Half-Wave Plate (HWP)
Introduces a 180° (λ/2) phase retardation. Rotates the plane of linear polarization by twice the fast axis angle (2α). Converts LCP ↔ RCP. Equivalent to a mirror reflection of the polarization state. Highly useful for arbitrary polarization rotation.
Γ = 180° (λ/2)
Rotates pol. by 2α
LCP ↔ RCP
M = R(-α)·diag(-i, +i)·R(α)
05
🔍 Analyzer
Identical to a polarizer, but used as the detection element. Rotating the analyzer while measuring intensity reveals the polarization state of the incoming beam. A complete 360° rotation sweep gives the Malus' Law curve for that state.

Section 03

MALUS'
LAW

The cornerstone of polarization optics. States how intensity changes when linearly polarized light passes through a rotated analyzer.

I(θ) = I₀ · cos²(θ) — Interactive: animating analyzer rotation
I = I₀ · cos²(θ)
Where I₀ is the initial intensity after the first polarizer, and θ is the angle between the polarizer transmission axis and the analyzer axis.

▸ At θ = 0°: I = I₀ (maximum, parallel polarizers)
▸ At θ = 90°: I = 0 (minimum, crossed polarizers)
▸ At θ = 45°: I = I₀/2 (half intensity)
📐
Physical Intuition
Think of the E-field as a vector with magnitude E₀. The analyzer only passes the component along its axis — a projection. If the angle between them is θ, the transmitted amplitude is E₀·cosθ. Since intensity ∝ amplitude², we get I = I₀·cos²θ.
🔄
Effect of Wave Plates
With QWP: Linear → Circular polarization. The cos²θ curve flattens to a nearly uniform intensity regardless of analyzer angle.

With HWP: Rotates the polarization axis by 2α. The cos²θ curve shifts horizontally by 2α.

Our simulation shows all three curves simultaneously in the Hardware mode.
📊
Experimental Verification
In the lab, we rotated the analyzer in 5° steps from 0° to 360° and recorded the photodetector voltage. The resulting curve closely matched the theoretical cos²θ prediction, validating our optical alignment and component quality.
Section 04

STOKES
PARAMETERS

A complete description of any polarization state using four real-valued measurable quantities — including partially polarized light that Jones vectors cannot describe.

S₀
Total Intensity
|Ex|² + |Ey|²
S₁
H vs V linear
|Ex|² − |Ey|²
S₂
±45° linear
2·Re(Ex·Ey*)
S₃
Circular
2·Im(Ex·Ey*)
🌐
Poincaré Sphere
Normalizing by S₀ gives (s₁, s₂, s₃) — a point on or inside a unit sphere called the Poincaré sphere. North pole = RCP. South pole = LCP. Equator = all linear polarization states. Any point on the surface = fully polarized. Interior = partially polarized.
🔬
Why Stokes?
Jones vectors only work for fully coherent light. Stokes parameters are measurable intensities — you can determine them directly from 4–6 polarimetric measurements with a polarizer and wave plate. This is what real instruments (polarimeters, ellipsometers) do in practice.
Section 05

OUR LAB
SETUP

How we built and conducted the experiment — from optical bench alignment to data collection and analysis.

Schematic: Optical bench layout used in our experiment
01
🔴 Laser Alignment
We set up a 650 nm diode laser on an optical rail, ensuring the beam was level and well-collimated. The first step was aligning all elements to be co-linear with the beam path. A screen at the far end confirmed alignment.
02
🔆 Polarizer Placement
A calibrated linear polarizer (extinction ratio > 1000:1) was mounted first in the beam path. We set it to 0° (horizontal) as the reference. The polarizer degree scale was verified by maximizing and minimizing transmission.
03
⬡ Wave Plate Insertion
QWP and HWP mounts were placed after the polarizer. Wave plates were oriented by finding the extinction condition (rotating until the output flicked between max and min). The fast axis was identified by the minimum reading.
04
🔍 Analyzer Rotation
The rotating analyzer was set on a precision rotation stage. We swept from 0° to 360° in 5° increments, recording the photodetector voltage at each step. All readings were normalized to the maximum value.
05
📡 Detection & Recording
A silicon photodetector (Thorlabs PDA36A) connected to a multimeter recorded intensity as voltage. Data was logged into a spreadsheet, then compared against the theoretical Jones calculus predictions from our simulation.
06
📊 Analysis & Simulation
Experimental curves were overlaid with our Streamlit simulation output. We compared Malus' Law curves, polarization ellipses, and Stokes parameters. Agreement within ≈ 3% confirmed accurate alignment and validated our Jones calculus engine.

What We Understood
Jones Calculus Works
The 2×2 Jones matrix formalism accurately predicts the output polarization state for any combination of linear optical elements. Our experimental results matched the simulation to within 3%, confirming both our alignment and the mathematical model.
Wave Plates are Powerful
A quarter-wave plate can completely change the character of polarization — converting linear to circular. This is the basis of many real optical instruments: 3D cinema glasses, LCDs, optical fiber sensors, and quantum communication systems all rely on this principle.
Stokes is Measurable
Unlike Jones vectors (which are mathematical abstractions), Stokes parameters are direct intensity measurements. We computed them from our detector readings and confirmed they correctly indicated circular vs. linear states — a key real-world polarimetry technique.
